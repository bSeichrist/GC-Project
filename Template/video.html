Digital Video

Exigence: The purpose of this project was to develop a short digital video intended for a web delivery of a possible cognate area for other MAPC students. For my group project, we covered the cognate area of technical writing and technical communication. We were to interview people that were familiar with this topic and discuss different aspects of the career field and courses taught at Clemson University. Lastly, we were to go over the Bureau of Labor Statistics data to support our reasoning for seeking a cognate in this field. In this project, we used lights, cameras, microphones, green screens, Adobe Photoshop, Camtasia, Snagit, and Adobe Premiere to develop our final product that would be uploaded to YouTube when completed.

Audience: The audience of this project was the class and other students seeking a higher degree in the Clemson MAPC program to persuade them into the cognate area of technical writing. Furthermore, this video could be used to communicate with prospective students that are interested in the program to convince them of the benefits of seeking a master’s degree at Clemson University.

Constraints: The constraints for this project required us to create a video that was roughly 5-minutes long that provided enough information to our audience to convince them to be interested in this major. We were also restricted to use royalty-free music and photos to support our project.

About The Project

Overview: My group consisted of Doris Xue Ding, Sarah Shahnovsky, and I, and we chose technical writing as our cognate area to do a video on. Doris and I are both interested in careers in technical writing, so we both thought it would be a good idea to learn more about this cognate area. Furthermore, I was already familiar with how fast the job market was growing towards technical writing, so I knew this would be a greatly persuasive point in our project.

Pre-Interview Questions: Before we decided who to interview, we had to discuss and come up with a list of questions that we would like answered. When we were coming up with questions, we decided that the more questions we asked the better. In doing so, we would have more than enough content, and we could cut out the information we did not needed to later. The questions we asked were based on our brainstorm to come up with as many questions as possible:
              1. How are you involved with technical writing?
              2. In your opinion, what is a technical writer?
              3. Where do you think technical writing is going as the future depends more on technology?
              4. What digital experience do you think we need to cover in order to be prepared for this field?
              5. What courses could someone take for this to be their cognate area?
              6. What kind of technical writing do you expect your students to do in your courses?
              7. Are there any certifications or clubs that we should belong to?
              8. How should people prepare to get a job in this field?
              9. What is the work environment like?
             10. What makes this job appealing?
             11. What is the job market like for the technical writing industry?
             12. Is there anything that we missed or anything else you would like to say about technical writing?

       With these questions set, we decided to interview two professors at Clemson University that are teaching technical writing this fall. We hoped that they could give us prime examples of their teaching style and could stress what and why their field is important to teach to everyone, not just graduate students. We also hoped that these professors could emphasize the need for technical writers and to support an interaction of disciplines. We emailed Professor Brian Adam Smith and Instructor William Pulley our questions and scheduled interviews.

Interview with Brian Adam Smith: We decided our first interview would be with one of our professors that is currently teaching technical writing and one that could help us with setting up the equipment in the Adobe Green Room. Professor Brian Adam Smith (BAS for short) is our professor for Workplace Communications, but we mainly wanted to interview him for his view on technical writing. Unlike my previous experience with technical writing, BAS likes to discuss the use of movies in the field of technical writing. His modern take on the field reflected the growing interest we saw in the field that showed technological advances being used to enhance how we give instructions and provide information to a large audience. Similar to the how-to videos we find on YouTube, instructional videos are a form of technical writing. We believed there was a negative stigma attached to technical writing of it being boring or being strictly text. Therefore, we wanted to show the other aspect of the technical writing field—the visual. It is quaint that we are doing a video project about the visual aspect of technical writing.


    When setting up the cameras, we went with the rule of thirds to make sure that BAS was not looking directly at the camera but beside the camera. He helped us adjust the lighting to make sure we were getting as little shadow as possible, and even wore a hat to help with his blond hair when it came to the green screen effects. Our interview left us with over 30 minutes of film, so we knew we would have to cut much of the video, but we got the answers we were looking for. His take on what a technical writer is and where he thinks technology is going as the field grows gave us clear insight into the use of film for scientific discovery and how this was a form of technical writing. We wanted to show that if a person wanted to pursue technical writing at Clemson, they would be met with all the technological advances that Clemson had to offer. Students would not be limited to working with only written text. After learning more about the Adobe softwares, we discovered that technical writing is much more than just how to write information. There is a very large visual component to the writing, so we decided that his response about the visual aspect was perfect for our project.

Interview with William Pulley: Instructor Pulley is another technical writing instructor at Clemson University, and he was very helpful in our interview as well. When interviewing Mr. Pulley, we also had over 30 minutes of footage, but we would only end up using one minute of this footage because he answered the one question we needed to answer perfectly: What kind of technical writing do you expect your students to do in your courses?

        When answering this question, he explained that technical writing was a discipline that could not be tied to just one field of expertise because every field needs technical writers. We thought this was a very powerful message to send to people interested in this cognate area. By stating that he has engineers, biologists, and other students in his classes, he showed that this is a very flexible cognate area to pursue. Furthermore, he discusses the different kinds of documents that students would learn to write in his program: instructional videos, information pamphlets, etc. We decided to use this question from Pulley because his message shows that a person with a cognate of technical writing can choose the aspect of industry they wish to pursue because all fields of industry need a technical writer. This is a huge appeal to people because it gives people options at other cognate areas may not. For example, if you wanted to pursue a career in health communications, you are pretty much limited to that field. If you want to be a technical writer, you could write documents for NASA or work for IKEA making instruction manuals. We just wanted to show that the normal constraints were not there.

Story Board: After all of the information was taken down, we had to edit and create a story-board that would provide all the information we needed. We began with the basic question of how to define a technical writer. To come up with a definition, we decided to begin with the Society for Technical Communications—the largest technical writing society—to find a definition. Their definition was broad enough for our demands, so we created a page layout in Adobe Photoshop, and we used Adobe Premiere to make the list scroll in the video by altering the position of the panel over a period of time.

        We wanted to back the Society for Technical Communications (STC) definition with the definition of BAS to show how the definition applied to classes about technical writing at Clemson University. His definition was broad, but it showed how the world relies on technical writing for figuring out how to do processes and create documents. We followed this up with Pulley, who had a slightly different understanding of technical writing, but it was valuable because it showed various forms of technical writing that BAS did not discuss.

        The last interview question we used was the use of video to create technical writing documents from BAS. He had prepared an example from history of the first use of film to figure out if a horse’s feet ever left the ground at the same time, and this provided us with a powerful understanding of how video production can be used in technical writing. We felt that this was very powerful aspect of technical writing that we have been using in our classes, so we made sure to have this part stand out by transitioning the background to an animated .gif of the scene he was depicting.

        We decided to end the video with one of our strongest points, the Bureau of Labor Statistics information that showed that technical writing is a growing industry and that the financial stability in this field is greater than many of the other fields of writing that you find.

        We used the positive questions about the job to ask the audience, “Now don’t you want to be a technical writer?” We meant it to be rhetorical, but it is also a reminder that this video is meant as a persuasion piece to join Clemson University and to pursue technical writing as a cognate area.

Transition Panels: Figuring out the transitions was difficult because we were not sure how we wanted to transition between the questions. We decided a silent zoom-in on the questions laid out in a checkerboard pattern would help the audience mentally transition between questions by giving a breaking period and visually transporting them into different parts of the interviews. Doris created the design for the questions.

Introduction: The introduction panel that states “technical writing” was created by Doris in Camtasia, and sound effects were added to create the clip. After this quick introduction, we transitioned to the STC definition of technical communications to support our claims about what they do.

Background: Adding the background to the green screen required us to use Adobe Premiere to accomplish. In Premiere, we used the Ultra Key to crop around the image we wanted to portray, and made the background black for when we had an image to place in the background. I created the background in Photoshop from a group of technical writing samples from BAS class on technical writing. Each picture was a screenshot from various students taking technical writing at the time, and it emphasizes the fact that technical writing expands across different fields. In the one photo, we have images of engineers using CAD, information planners, biologists, and electrical engineers writing out math equations in a video form to instruct people on how to complete a technical task. All of the photos were taken using the Snagit free-trial by the same people that make Camtasia. I designed the image and we felt that this image encapsulated our ideas and goals for technical writing.
        The transition to the .gif correlates with BAS discussing Edward Muybridge creating the first film to discover how horses feet left the ground as they ran. The clip is only two-seconds, so we had to copy and paste the .gif after we had positioned it on the screen correctly. After our class presentation, we were informed that this .gif played for too long, so we created a transition back to the original background to conclude our project.

Editing the Video: My biggest contribution to the project was editing the video. In this project, I was in charge of compiling the footage, images, and backgrounds, and it was my job to position all of this on the video.

       I learned how to make the panels move by changing the position of the image, so it scrolled as a person eyes followed along the writing, and how to zoom in on the transition panels with the questions to guide the audience beyond just a static page. Much of my work was in resizing the still images to be in-line with the video we were using and developing the backgrounds to fit the video screen that was already set. We decided as a group that a static page was too boring for an introduction, so all of our text moves to create a more visual experience that makes the viewer continuously follow along with the information.


       When shortening the videos to reduce the “ummms” and “ahhhs” we found that we had to add transitions—usually a fade to film—because the positions of the people talking would change. In my mind, I imagined that people would remain static while they spoke, but people move their hands, their heads, and change facial expressions as they talk, so it was impossible to cut between answers without transitions. In the end, we decided to leave some of the “ummms” and “ahhhs” to give our video a natural flow without too many interruptions.

        Throughout the video, I added transitions, typically a fade to film effect, to transition between the question panels to the interview responses to show a subtle transition to someone answering the question. To transition back to the question panels, we used a barndoor effect because it was strong in communicating a cut. I ended the video with a fade to black cut. For the audio, we had to raise the audio for BAS because it was too soft, and I had to do a small audio gain and fade when exiting the interview recordings because the white noise at the end was obvious against the background music. The background music had transitions at the beginning where it was louder, into the video where it was a low background noise. We decided to transition our background music when we reached the final part of our interviews where BAS discusses the invention of film in a technical writing aspect because we wanted to show a change of pace in the presentation. His interview reflected an in-depth analysis of the use of technical writing in one aspect, so we wanted it to stand out from the rest of the film. Towards the end, we had to repeat the song, but the fade-in made the audio sound perfect.

Critical Reflection

        The digital video was not my first time using Adobe Premiere, but it was my first time interviewing people and producing video that I edited. My previous times on Premiere were using basic panels with transitions and adding audio to these transitions. Our first failure was when we communicated with the professors about doing interviews. We failed to mention that the videos were to be under 10 minutes, so when we interviewed, many of the answers could not be used because they were simply too long. When it came to editing, we began with 30 to 40 minutes of recording from each professor. In total we used roughly 4 minutes and 30 seconds of the video in our final project. This meant making tons of edits and clipping the audio in the right spots and keeping track of where we would need transitions.This was a struggle to correlate the film with our storyboard based on the questions we asked, but we were able to develop a story line that only required us to make two text-heavy panels of information that we split up--one at the introduction and one at the end. This was done to create a balance throughout our video, and it was a mental cue for the audience that things were coming to an end. We also added movement to these pictures to make the reader follow along actively, rather than blankly stare at text.

        Through this project, we learned how to use Adobe Premiere and how to create a user-experience in video. In Photoshop, we created the backgrounds, the question board, and the textual panels, but in Premiere we made these still images come to life to direct the viewers through the video. This took a lot of practice with scaling and positioning effects in Premiere, but we were able to scale and position all the pertinent information to the center of the screen with slow enough movement to allow the viewer to read all the text of the document. To test the ability to read the information, we would edit the scaling and positioning then play it to make sure we could read the information in enough time. To aid the reader in following the text, we added audio of Sarah summarizing the text, so if the viewer decided not to read, they would still get the gist. I wish Star Wars would do something similar with their text.

        This project was a compilation of all my previous projects into one video. Using Camtasia and Photoshop, we were able to create visuals that would supplement the information that we would not get on film and we used them to provide transitions. Using what I learned in Audition, I was able to fade the audio in and out, and I knew how to change the audio, so I could layer background music into the interviews. I would have liked to have more time to work on this project, but we were under time constraints. However, I understand that we would not have been able to complete this project without having gained experience through the other projects.I believe that working as a team on this project made us better because we each came into the project knowing different things, and I believed I learned more from this project than all of the others.
